What's the Problem?

Home page

The global demand for the products of mining continually increases. Extraction of the metals and minerals from the raw mined ores produce a significant quantity of waste material in the form of tailings which are composed of crushed rock and the processing fluids from processing plants that remain after the extraction of economic metals, minerals, mineral fuels, or coal. The volume of tailings is normally far greater than the resource which was liberated from the raw ore and the tailings often contain potentially hazardous contaminants (D. Kossoff, 2014).
It is therefore a priority for responsible and reasonable mining organizations to proactively isolate the tailings such that they forestall them from entering groundwaters, rivers, lakes, and the wind and to control for possible chemical changes as a function of their storage. Considering expense and convenience, mining organizations have resorted to the use of tailings dams to store these tailings indefinitely. Tailings dams are some of the largest earth structures constructed by geotechnical engineers and to save costs the dams are constructed with the mining waste products and experts are not fully consulted or their recommendations are not adhered to.
Consequences An estimated 3 500 active tailings dams can be found around the globe of these there was between 2 and 5 major tailings dam failures together with 35 minor failures each year as of 2013 which has doubled in the past 10 years putting communities and environments at astronomical risk (Azam, 2010).

Dam Problems

Tailings dams are notoriously dangerous, and the World Mine Tailings Failures (WMTF) predicts 19 very serious dam failures before 2027. These colossal structures are built without major changes to law, regulation, or to industry practices, as well as without new technology to possibly reduce risk and increase loss control. The colossal size of these dams adds to the risk they pose, the Maravilhas II dam in Brazil stands 90m high and has been built up over 20 years (Vale, 2022). The common altering of these dams throughout their lifespans, with the dams generally being enlarged as the mine life is extended, without proper consultation and reinforcement increases the possibility that conditions could change or that supervision will fail. There is also little consequence for non-compliance with laws and regulations concerning tailings dams
Dam Problems Dam failure releases toxic substances such as arsenic, mercury, lead, cyanide, Sulphur, radionuclides, and other heavy metals usually into the natural water systems such as nearby rivers and lakes which increases the spread of the toxic substances. These toxic substances also leech into the ground water and topsoil causing long-term damage to water sources, food production, natural fauna and flora and human settlements. Solutions

The separation of solids and liquids provides the basis of resolving the hazards of slimes dams. Solid liquid separation does pose challenges particularly for fine and ultrafine particulate matter suspended in liquid. In a competitive market it is critical to keep operation costs as low as possible while still maintaining the safety and integrity of the communities and environments surrounding the mining site. Common filtration Options Historical solid liquid separation options such as belt filters, Drum filters, thickeners, disk filters, pan filters, and centrifuges unfortunately together with their high operational cost and foot space, none of these can handle the large quantities of slurry effluent produced by the processing mills nor are they able to produce a sufficiently dry product that can be safely stockpiled or stored with out additional costs. Filter Presses The solution is one that is both new and old being used in the Shang Dynasty in 1600B.C. This solution works across all mining mill tailings, paper mills and even sewage. The working principle of filter presses is that slurry is pumped into the machine such that solids are distributed evenly during the fill cycle. Solids build up on the filter cloth, forming the filter cake; the filtrate exits the filter plates through the corner ports into the manifold, yielding clean filtered water. Filter presses are a pressure filtration method and as such, as the filter press feed pump builds pressure, solids build within the chambers until they are completely chock-full of solids, forming the cake. Once the chambers are full, the cycle is complete, and the filter cakes are ready to be released. In many higher capacity filter presses, fast action automatic plate shifters are employed, speeding cycle time. Filter presses have a simple operation procedure which can be fully automatized, they also have the lowest capital and operational expense requirements of all filtration systems and even slurry dams. Benefits Plate and frame filters provide the options of further beneficiation of valuable ores and minerals from the previous waste stream increasing product yield and profit. Filtrate from the filtration can be recycled back into the processing mill significantly reducing the raw water requirement and preserving natural water sources. The solids form dense and stable filter cakes with low moisture content which are easily stackable and movable allowing for backfilling of previously mined sections reducing the costs of rehabilitation. With governments being pressured to impose stricter regulations with regards to waste management of mining mills the choice is clear: plate and frame filtration will not only reduce the impact of mining on the communities and environments surrounding them they will also produce profit for the mining companies.
