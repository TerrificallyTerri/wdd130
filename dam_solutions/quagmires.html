Damming Situation

Tailings dams are notoriously dangerous, and the World Mine Tailings Failures (WMTF) predicts 19 very serious dam failures before 2027. The dams are built without major changes to law, regulation, industry practices, or new technology. to possibly reduce risk and increase loss control. The colossal size of these dams adds to the risk they pose. The Maravilhas II dam in Brazil stands 90m high and has been built up over 20 years (Vale, 2022). The common practice of altering of these dams throughout their lifespans, with the dams generally being enlarged as the mine life is extended, without proper consultation and reinforcement increases the possibility that conditions could change or that supervision will fail. There is also little consequence for non-compliance with laws and regulations concerning tailings dams

Dam failures release toxic substances such as arsenic, mercury, lead, cyanide, Sulphur, radionuclides, and other heavy metals. Spills often drain into the natural water systems such as nearby rivers and lakes which increases the spread of the toxic substances. These toxic substances also leech into the ground water and topsoil causing long-term damage to water sources, food production, natural fauna and flora and human settlements.

Click on the dam names below for some of the known casualty figures of dam failures:

Mailuu-Suu

Kyrgyzstan, 1958

Fatalities: 100 000 - 300 000 and ongoing

Uncontrolled release of 600,000 cubic metres (21,000,000 cu ft) of the radioactive uranium-mine tailings. The effects are still being felt in this area.
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1958_Mailuu-Suu_tailings_dam_failureKyrgyzstan

Aberfan

Wales, 1966

Fatalities: 144

Coal mine tailings spill tip collapsed engulfing Pantglas Junior School and natural springs. Greater portion of casualties were young children.
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aberfan_disaster

Buffalo Creek

Logan County USA, 1972

Fatalities: 125 Injured: 1121

4000 left homeless as 551 homes were destroyed. Members of the oversight committee were sympathetic to the coal industry and might have been complicit in the genesis of the flood. The mine was fined 1 million dollars and allowed to continue with activites.

It took 50 years before rehabilitation of the area was sufficient to resume previous tourist activities.
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buffalo_Creek_flood

Val di Stava Dam

Stava, Italy, 1985

Fatalities: 268

Dams were poorly maintained resulting in 180 000 cubic meters of slurry travelling at 90km/h for 4.2km destroying the village and everything in its path to the Avisio River polluting it.
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Val_di_Stava_dam_collapse

Merrispruit

Virginia South Africa, 1994

Fatalities: 17

A 2.5m high wave containing cyanides and lead of 600 000m3 engulfed 80 homes 300m from the dam. Continuous construction, negligence and oversight reduction due to expense led to the failure of the dam.
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merriespruit_tailings_dam_disaster

Mariana

Minas Gerais, Brazil, 2015

Fatalities: 19-official estimate

43.7million m3 tailings containing high concentrations of heavy metals, arsenic, lead and mercury destroyed 620km down river including entire fish populations in the Doce River. This failure compromised the water source for 230 municipalities, subsistence farming all along the river, 1,469 hectares of riparian forest and the southern Bahia littoral zone and marine biodiversity sanctuary in the Atlantic Ocean. Mariana disaster caused a humanitarian crisis due to the displacement of people. The dam failed after reports of structural compromise in 2013 which were not remedied.
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mariana_dam_disaster

Brumadinho Dam

Minas Gerais, Brazil, 2019

Fatalities: 270-official estimate

Dam owned by Vale the same company involved in the Mariana Dam disaster. Sensors monitoring the dams structural integrity had problems – this was known by Vale. Failure resulted in the pollution of the Paraopeba River. 18 of Vales dams have no guarantee of stability. Brazil’s weak regulatory structures and gaps in countries' regulations allow for these types of failures to continue.
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brumadinho_dam_disaster
